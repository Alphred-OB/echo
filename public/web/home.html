Security model

Questions worth asking

The honest answers, including the limits.

What if someone records the sound and replays it?
Nothing happens. Every sign-in plays a different sound — a fresh random 96-bit code that is destroyed the moment it is used and expires after 30 seconds anyway. A recording is a ticket that has already been torn. This exact attack is part of our automated test suite, and it fails every time.
What if someone steals my phone?
They still need to get past your phone's own lock to open Echo Key and approve anything — and every sign-in shows an explicit approve / deny prompt. From any other signed-in computer you can revoke the stolen phone in one click, instantly turning its key into junk.
What if I lose or change my phone?
Sign in with one of your single-use recovery codes (generated from your dashboard, stored only as hashes), add the new phone by scanning a QR code, and revoke the old one. Recovery attempts are rate-limited to five per 15 minutes.
Does it work in a noisy room?
Usually yes — voices, music, and traffic live far below the 18–20 kHz band Echo uses, and the encoding carries error correction. If the environment or hardware does struggle, there is an audible fallback mode that trades silence for robustness.
What does the server actually store?
Your username, your devices' public keys, and hashed recovery codes. No passwords exist anywhere — not hashed, not encrypted, not at all. A complete database breach gives an attacker nothing they can sign in with.
What are the known limits?
A live relay attack — streaming the sound in real time to an accomplice near your phone — is mitigated by the approve prompt and ultrasound's short range, but not yet by cryptographic distance bounding. We say so openly; security products that claim no limits have stopped being honest.
